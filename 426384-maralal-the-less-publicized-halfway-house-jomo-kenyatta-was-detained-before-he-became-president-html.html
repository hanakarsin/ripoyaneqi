Maralal: Historic Halfway House Jomo Kenyatta Was Detained Before He Became President

 Posted on January 1, 0001  |  3 minutes  |  636 words  |  Hettie Henneman
  • The Maralal halfway house is a historic piece in Jomo Kenyatta's period of detention as it was where he spent his last months before becoming President
  • Furthermore, it is in the same house that the current President, Uhuru Kenyatta, was born in 1961
  • Mzee Jomo Kenyatta's book, Facing Mount Kenya, was also not only written in the same house but also derives the title from its location

The books of History record that Kenya's founding President Mzee Jomo Kenyatta was detained in different jails in the harsh districts of Northern Kenya namely Kapenguria, Lokitaung, and Lodwar.

However, the most memorable is his house arrest stint in Maralal where he was allowed the freedom of living with family, moving freely within the town, and interacting with locals.

"By that time the British had realised that Mzee Kenyatta was going to be Kenya's leader despite their plans to entice other leaders to form a government in his absence," the presidential museum records.

Pictures of Kenyatta in Maralal show him looking more relaxed, in more instances strolling around town with a bodyguard in tow.

His detention in Maralal lasted only five months but it remained the most eventful and memorable stints of his extended period of detention in different locations.

Most documented period

What many Kenyans may not be familiar with, the presidential museum records is that Kenyatta's detention in Maralal was different from the others.

The British chose Maralal as a halfway house where they would keep him during his last months in detention after a stalemate by KANU's insistence that his release should precede Kenya's independence.

Kenya's first Vice President Jaramogi Oginga Odinga, who, together with Tom Mboya and Mbiyu Koinange was at the helm of KANU, led calls for delaying Kenya's independence until Kenyatta was freed.

"In preparation for Mzee Kenyatta's transfer, the British hurriedly constructed a three-bedroom house in Maralal. A doctor was also posted to the town and assigned to serve Mzee Kenyatta," records indicate.

As it happened, it is during his period at the Maralal house that his exploits in incarceration were most documented.

Furthermore, his first media interview since being arrested in 1952 happened in Maralal where he was accompanied by his daughter Margaret.

Kenyatta House

This was also the one place that Kenyatta received the most number of delegations from Kenya and abroad.

Apart from the leadership aspect, other important milestones in his own family happened during the time.

First, his daughters Jenny and Kristina started schooling at Maralal DEB School, and Uhuru Kenyatta, who is now the President of Kenya, was born in 1961.

The house soon became known as Kenyatta House.

Facing Mount Kenya, a famous book by the founding father was also authored when he lived in Maralal, with the title said to have been inspired by the location of the house from where one can see the tip of the mountain on clear days.

Freedom, at last

On August 14, 1961, the colonial government moved Kenyatta to his Gatundu home where he was put under watch and barred from making political statements.

A week later, on August 21, Kenyatta was finally given his full freedom.
